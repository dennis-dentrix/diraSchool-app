# How a Ruiru School Cut Report Card Time From 3 Days to 2 Hours — and Recovered KES 800,000 in Fee Arrears

*This is the story of a private primary school in Ruiru, Kiambu County, with 310 students across Grade 1 to Grade 9 (JSS). Details have been kept general at the school's request.*

---

## Term 3, 2023: The Breaking Point

Margaret had been the head teacher at her school in Ruiru for six years. She was proud of what they had built — a stable staff, improving CBC results, and a parent community that trusted them.

But every end of term, she dreaded the same two weeks.

Her secretary and two class teachers would spend the final days of term locked in the staffroom, reformatting report cards in Microsoft Word. One teacher used a different template. Another left fields blank. Margaret would review 310 cards, find errors in a third of them, send batches back for correction. The printer would jam. The internet would cut out mid-download. They'd miss the official last day of term by two days, sending parents home without cards and explaining the delay over WhatsApp.

Meanwhile, the bursar was fighting a different battle. Forty-three families had outstanding fee balances from the previous term. Some disputed what they owed — their records showed a payment the bursar couldn't find in the ledger. Two parents had actually paid, but the M-Pesa notification had been missed during a busy collection day. The school absorbed those disputes quietly rather than push good families away. Another twelve parents had rolled over balances so many times that nobody was sure of the correct figure anymore.

"I was spending Monday mornings chasing money instead of running a school," Margaret said. "And term-end felt like a crisis every single time — three years in a row."

In November 2023, after another chaotic final week, she decided something had to change.

---

## Finding the Right System

Margaret didn't rush the decision. She'd heard about school management software before — a colleague in Nairobi used one but complained it didn't support CBC properly and the reports had to be reformatted before printing. Another school in her area had tried a free system, liked the price, but found the support nonexistent when things went wrong.

She made a list of what she actually needed:

- CBC report cards that printed correctly without reformatting — all seven learning areas, the right four-level rubric
- Fee tracking that matched M-Pesa payments automatically
- Something her secretary and bursar could learn quickly — neither was highly technical
- A parent portal so families could check their own balances without calling the office
- A support team she could call when something went wrong, not just email

She trialled DiraSchool in December 2023, during the school holiday — before committing to using it in Term 1.

"I wanted to see the report card generator myself. I entered marks for one class and ran the cards. They came out correctly — the rubric was right, the learning areas were right, the format was what CBC expects. That was the moment I knew."

---

## Term 1, 2024: Going Live

DiraSchool's onboarding team migrated the school's student list from their Excel file in the first week of January. 310 students, guardian contacts, class assignments, and fee structures were loaded before the term started.

The first week was not without friction.

Two teachers forgot their login passwords on day one. The bursar spent an afternoon learning how to set up the fee structure — the school had six different fee line items (tuition, activity, lunch, uniform, exam, and development levy) and she wanted them set up correctly before recording any payments. One parent called Margaret directly asking why they'd received an SMS about a portal they hadn't signed up for.

"There were small problems in week one. But the support team picked up the phone every time I called. That made all the difference."

By week two, the daily attendance process had changed completely. Teachers marked attendance on their phones or laptops at 8am. The dashboard showed Margaret which classes had submitted and which hadn't by 8:30am. Absent students triggered an SMS to their guardian automatically.

"Before, I'd walk around checking registers at 9am. Now I open the dashboard. If a class hasn't submitted, I send the teacher a message. It takes five minutes instead of forty-five."

---

## The Fee Transformation

The biggest change in Term 1 wasn't the reports or the attendance. It was the fees.

The school uses M-Pesa as their primary payment channel. Parents pay to a school paybill number. Previously, the bursar monitored M-Pesa notifications on her phone, then manually updated the Excel ledger. Payments made outside school hours — evenings, weekends — would pile up and be entered in batches, sometimes a week late.

With DiraSchool's M-Pesa C2B integration, every payment matched automatically to the student's account within seconds of the parent paying. The bursar received a notification. A receipt was generated and stored. The parent's portal updated immediately.

"In the first month, three parents who had disputed their balances in the previous term came to ask about their current balance. I showed them their payment history on the screen — every payment, every date, every amount. Two of them paid immediately. One apologised. There were no more disputes after that."

By the end of Term 1, only 11 families had outstanding balances — down from 43 the previous term. The bursar's weekly time on fee-related work dropped from 15 hours to 5 hours.

---

## End of Term 1: The Report Card Moment

The real test came at the end of Term 1, 2024.

Class teachers had been entering results throughout the term — after each assessment, in the system, on their own time. No batch entry panic at the end. No shared spreadsheet. When the end of term arrived, most of the data was already in.

On the second-to-last day of term, Margaret asked all class teachers to finalise their results, add teacher remarks, and submit for review. By 3pm, all 11 classes had submitted.

Margaret reviewed the cards that evening. Added her principal remarks. Approved the batch.

The following morning — the last day of term — the secretary printed 310 report cards before noon.

"I stood in the staffroom and watched them come out of the printer. Every card correct. The right format, the right rubric, the right learning areas. No corrections. We gave them out to parents at pickup and finished before 3pm.

That had never happened in six years."

## What Margaret Would Tell Another Head Teacher

We asked Margaret what she'd say to a head teacher still running on Excel and WhatsApp.

"I'd tell them that the problems they're dealing with feel normal because they've always been there. The late report cards, the fee disputes, the calls about balances — you stop seeing them as problems and start seeing them as just how it is.

They're not how it has to be.

The switch is not as hard as it looks. We were nervous about moving our data, training the staff, learning something new. It took about two weeks to feel comfortable. By week three it was just how we worked.

The thing I didn't expect was what it did for my confidence as a principal. When a parent asks me about their child's fees, I know the answer is accurate. When a teacher asks when results are due, I know the process is the same for everyone. When a new term starts, I'm not dreading the admin — I'm focused on the school.

That's what I didn't know I was missing."
