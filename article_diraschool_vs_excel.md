# Why Kenyan Schools Are Ditching Excel for a Proper School System

If you walked into most Kenyan private schools today and asked to see their student records, you'd find some version of the same setup: a shared Excel file on the secretary's laptop, a WhatsApp group where teachers send attendance in the morning, a physical fee ledger behind the bursar's desk, and a folder of typed report cards that took two weeks to produce.

It works. Until it doesn't.

This article is not about selling you software. It's about what actually happens when a school keeps running on spreadsheets — and what changes when it stops.

---

## The Excel School: A Picture You'll Recognise

Imagine a school of 280 students in Kiambu. Well-run. Good teachers. Principal who knows every child by name.

Here's how a typical term looks:

**Week 1:** The secretary spends three days rebuilding the student list because last term's Excel file got corrupted. A teacher accidentally saved over it. There's no version history.

**Midterm:** The bursar is chasing 47 fee arrears. She sends WhatsApp messages to parents one by one. Some parents claim they paid — the bursar has no way to verify quickly because M-Pesa messages are buried in her phone and the ledger was updated by hand. Three parents dispute their balance. Two of them are right.

**End of term:** The class teachers spend the last week typing report cards. Each teacher does their own format. The principal asks for a consistent template — so three teachers redo their cards from scratch. The school prints 280 report cards, discovers 12 have errors, reprints. The secretary works until 8pm on the last day of term.

**New term:** A parent calls asking what fees are owed. The bursar pulls up the Excel file, finds three different versions, opens the wrong one, gives the wrong figure. The parent arrives with the wrong amount. There's a dispute at the gate on opening day.

None of this is a failure of the people involved. The secretary is competent. The bursar is diligent. The principal is excellent. **The tools are the problem.**

## What Excel Actually Costs Your School

Schools rarely calculate the real cost of manual systems. It doesn't show up as a line item. But it's there.

### Time

A school bursar tracking fees manually spends 15+ hours per week on payment reconciliation, balance queries, and overdue follow-up. That's nearly two full working days every week — on one task that should take an hour.

A class teacher with 40 students spends 4–6 hours per term entering results into multiple systems: their personal gradebook, a shared class sheet, and the report card template. Enter the same data three times. Check it three times. Find the error on the fourth check.

A school secretary generating 280 report cards manually — formatting, checking, printing, correcting — loses 3 full days at the end of every term. Three terms a year. Nine days a year on report cards alone.

**Across a school of 280 students: conservatively 40+ hours of avoidable admin work per term.** That's time your staff aren't spending on students, parents, or the actual work of running a school.

### Money

Schools lose fee revenue in two ways with manual tracking: errors and disputes.

Errors happen when a payment is received, recorded in one place, and missed in another. A parent pays KES 15,000 via M-Pesa. The bursar sees the M-Pesa notification, marks it in her phone, intends to update the ledger later, and forgets. The ledger still shows a balance. The parent gets a late-payment SMS (which was sent manually, so it went to the wrong number anyway). Nobody wins.

Disputes happen when parents and the school disagree on what was paid. Without a timestamped, auditable payment history, both sides are guessing. Schools often absorb these disputes rather than damage a parent relationship. That's revenue quietly written off.

Conservative estimate: schools tracking fees manually lose 3–5% of fee income per term to errors and unresolved disputes. On a school collecting KES 14 million per year in fees, that's KES 420,000–700,000 per year. Enough to pay a teacher's salary.

### Trust

Parents today are used to seeing their bank balances in real time, tracking their NHIF contributions online, and getting M-Pesa confirmation in seconds. When their child's school can't tell them their balance without digging through a ledger, that contrast is jarring.

This isn't just a perception problem. Schools that can't provide clear, timely fee information get more disputes, more late payments, and more friction at term start. Parents who feel like the school "doesn't have it together" are also quicker to consider alternatives when a competitor school opens nearby.

## What Changes When You Replace Excel

Let's go back to the school in Kiambu. Same 280 students. Same staff. Different tools.

### Fee management becomes quiet

A parent pays fees via M-Pesa. Within seconds, the payment is matched automatically to their child's account. A receipt is generated and sent. The bursar's dashboard updates in real time. She can see, at any moment, exactly who has paid, who hasn't, and what the outstanding balance is — by class, by term, by student.

At the end of term, she doesn't spend days reconciling. She runs a report. It takes four minutes.

When a parent calls asking about their balance, the bursar pulls it up in 10 seconds while they're still on the phone. No ledger. No "let me call you back."

### Report cards take minutes, not days

The class teacher has been entering results throughout the term — after each assessment, in a few minutes, on their phone or laptop. The rubric is built in: EE, ME, AE, BE across all seven learning areas. No formatting. No template hunting.

At end of term, the teacher clicks "Generate report cards." In under two minutes, 40 report cards are ready to preview, print, or share digitally with parents. The principal reviews them online, adds remarks, and approves. Done before lunch on the last day of term — not at 8pm.

### Parents stop calling the office

Parents get SMS updates when their child is marked absent. They check their fee balance and payment history on their phone. When results are published, they see them in the parent portal. The phone at the front office stops ringing with balance queries and "when are results coming out?" calls.

One school's deputy principal put it this way: *"Parents used to call the office asking for results. Now they check the portal themselves. It's cut our admin calls by more than half."*

### The principal can see the whole school

Instead of asking the bursar for a summary (which takes an hour to pull together), the principal opens a dashboard. Who paid this term. Which class has the most outstanding fees. How many students were absent last week. What the average competency ratings look like across Grade 4.

This shifts the principal's week. Less time gathering information. More time acting on it.

## "But We've Managed Fine With Excel for Years"

This is the most common objection — and it's understandable. Your school is running. Students are learning. You've built systems around your tools, even imperfect ones.

A few honest responses:

**"Fine" is different from "well."** Your staff are managing, but at what cost? The hours spent on avoidable admin, the fee revenue quietly lost to errors, the report cards that took a week too long — these have become invisible because they've always been there. They feel normal. They're not inevitable.

**The gap is widening.** In 2019, parents expected a typed report card. In 2026, they expect an SMS when their child is absent, an app to check their balance, and a digital report card shared on their phone. Schools that can't deliver this are already behind the curve — and the gap widens every year.

In practice, a school of 280 students can be fully live on a new system in 2–3 weeks, with a vendor that provides hands-on onboarding. The data migration (your student list from Excel) takes a few days. Staff training for office and teachers takes a half-day. After that, the system does the work.

## What to Look For When You Make the Switch

Not all systems are equal. As you evaluate options, here are the questions that matter most for a Kenyan CBC school:

**Is CBC native or added on?** A system built for traditional curricula that "supports CBC" will have workarounds. A system built for CBC from the start will have the right rubric (EE/ME/AE/BE), the right learning areas (all seven), and report cards that print correctly without reformatting.

**How deep is the M-Pesa integration?** There's a big difference between "we support M-Pesa" and "payments auto-match to student accounts with zero manual entry." Ask to see the payment flow in a live demo.

**Does it work offline?** If your school loses internet for a day, can teachers still mark attendance and enter results? Data that syncs when connection returns is non-negotiable in Kenya.

**Who supports you when something breaks?** A system with a Nairobi-based team that answers calls during school hours is different from one with email-only support in a different time zone.

**Can you export your data?** Your student records, fee history, and academic results belong to your school. A vendor who won't commit to a clean data export is asking you to trust them indefinitely with no exit.
